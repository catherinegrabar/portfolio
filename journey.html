Journey

Overview

Journey was a 6 week design-led sprint at Bing. The goal was to define an end-to-end travel experience that can be built within 12 months. After the sprint's completion, my design sprint team pitched it to the Bing Maps leadership with the intent to prioritize building the app.

The Journey app is focused on the three stages of a trip: planning, travel and reminiscing. The experiences we focused on were creating personalized travel itineraries, encouraging discovery during a trip and creating a beautiful journal of the trip for the user to look back on or share.

App user stories

Planning your trip

To make travel planning less stressful, Journey uses Bing data to begin a personalized itinerary based on your travel needs and preferences (e.g. budget, pace, interests). From there, you customize the itinerary by adding and removing places. The goal of this stage is to help reduce the stress and effort needed to start planning a trip.

During your trip

Once on your trip, Journey helps you explore and learn about your surroundings. Suggestions of attractions you might like show up on the map as you're walking around. If you're tired and need to take a break, it's easy to find a coffee shop near you. When you're ready to continue to your next attraction, the app will provide walking, transit or driving directions to help you get there.

Creating this part of the experience was my main focus during phase 2 of the design sprint.

After your trip

After returning home, Journey puts together a beautiful travel journal. It is filled with your photos, notes and pinned map of the places you visited. This creates an easy way to look back on a trip as well as share it with friends or others who are traveling to that location.

Process

The design sprint was split into two phases. Phase one kicked off with 11 designers and in this first few weeks, we gathered inspiration, did competitive analysis, derived our favorite common elements from the moodboards and then organized them into themes. The three themes we developed were: My Trip (collections, itinerary, journal), Guide (city information) and Social (collaborating, sharing trip, sharing journal).

In addition in being an active contributor to the inspiration gathering and brainstorming, I was the Bing Maps website expert and shared my maps knowledge with the group.

We continued exploring ideas by sketching out examples of the experiences. Out of that process came the following key experiences: automagic itinerary creation, user seeding the type of trip, dynamic suggestions for changing the itinerary while on the go, dynamic map view and a beautiful visualization of the user's trip.

Phase 2 was reduced to 3 designers and it was all about aggregating all of the ideas and starting to design the product. We identified five scenarios we wanted to showcase as well as how they fell into the groupings of before, during and after the trip. We defined the scope of the app, constraints and design tenets. After many iterations of wireframes, prototypes, high-fidelity mocks and presentations, we concluded the design sprint.

After the designs were completed, we collaborated with our user researchers to put app prototypes into the lab. Much useful feedback was generated by these studies. As one example, we learned that users valued having readily visible information about travel costs for each destination.

Outcome

Building of the Journey app was never prioritized, but many of the design outcomes are being applied to other maps projects. Another positive outcome of the design sprint process was to help develop strong interpersonal relationships between team members. It increased respect for the design process from our project managers and software developers and it inspired the maps team with the possibilities of what we could build.
